Abstract

One of the biggest problems that we currently face is the enormous amount of plastic waste in the environment. These microplastics have disrupted global ecosystems due to their resistance to degradation and unchallenged consumption by organisms at all levels of the food chain. This problem is especially worrisome in the oceans where over 600 species of marine life are negatively affected by microplastics in the ocean.

Green Ocean’s aim is to genetically modify marine cyanobacteria that will enable it to degrade polyethylene terephthalate or PET, the most common form of plastic in the oceans. The engineered cyanobacteria harbor PETase, which is an enzyme that breaks down PET. Our approach is novel because instead of using the traditional e. coli, which may not survive in the ocean environment, cyanobacteria also known as “blue- green algae” are photosynthetic bacteria common in the ocean.

We accomplished this by modifying the prototypical Ideonella sakaiensis PETase gene to be compatible with expression and secretion in cyanobateria. This engineering was accomplished in a dual-host plasmid shuttle vector in E coli, and then transferred to a model cynaobacterium Synechococus elongatus by conjugation.We also developed a PET degradation assay system consisting of fluorescent PET nanoparticles. As the engineered cyanobacteria degraded the PET nanoparticles, the size change was measured by dynamic light scattering, fluorescent polarization imaging, and release of the fluorescent dye over time measured through a plate reader.

As a team we are concerned of this world problem and our final goal is to have cleaner oceans in our environment.
