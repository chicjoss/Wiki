Safety

Safety Training Access

Team members will all receive an IBC protocol included in the mandate Illinois Tech safety training, as well as project and lab specialize in training within a PI host lab. The team members will learn about topics in this IGEM through: Lab access rules, responsible individuals, good microbial technique, disinfection and sterilization, chemicals, fire and electrical safety. They will be working areas that have open benches if they are handing biological materials. The safety lab of our work space is BSL1.

Risk management

Personal protective equipment, access controls, accident reporting, waste management system, special procedures or protocols that address safety or security

Rules, training, containment

There are specific waste protocols in place for disposing of organic materials to ensure that there is no spread of contamination. Only those who have safety and lab training are allowed to be in the lab at any given time, and the PIs are always available to give answers when asked questions about protocol. Autoclaves are used for sterilization of waste material.

Goals

Our goal is to engineer an organism that will have a plasmid containing a gene that enables the organism to digest PET plastic. This organism will be developed from an cyanobacteria so that it can survive in aqueous environments in order to break down microplastics found in global water bodies.

Organisms

Escherichia coli, Synechococcus elongatus. Both of these are classified as BSL1 by the ATCC:

  • S.e.: https://www.atcc.org/Products/All/33912.aspx
  • E coli K12: https://www.atcc.org/products/all/25404.aspx

Have been approved for use in this project by IIT IBC (institutional biosafety committee)

Risks of Organisms

No significant risks of the following organisms:

E coli K12

E coli K12 strains are a non-pathogenic variant of normal intestinal flora in healthy individuals, and have been extensively used in laboratories throughout the world specifically because they are not significantly dangerous.

S elongatus

S elongatus is a common environmental cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) that is found in waterways. Indeed, many strains from the cyanobacteria repository we obtained it from. UTEX () were obtained from environmental water sources (although the type strain we are using was deposited). Cyanobacteria are free living non-pathogenic photosynthetic organisms already found in most environments.

Experiments in our organisms

We will modify an existing PETase (polyethylene terephthalate hydrolase) enzyme ( a plastic degrading enzyme) to:

  • Be compatible with expression in cyanobacteria
  • Will enhances its catalytic activity
  • Be secreted in cyanobacteria
  • Be easily detectable

Which will then allow us to clone it into a vector compatible with expression in out selected cyanobacteria.

This will then be evaluated in an assay where the degradation of plastic microparticles exposed to the experimental cyanobacteria will be measured by DLS or fluorescence

Safety, security or ethical risks

Clearly there would be huge objection to introduction of GMOs in the environment.Below will give you an idea:

  1. GMO argument: GMOs have largely been proved to be safe but there is still a lot of objection to them. So the fear is real even if unfounded and should be addressed
  2. Introduced species argument Is more of a real risk.there are many cases of humans screwing up an ecosystem by introducing a non-native species. And a few cases of doing a good job and fixing a problem well (less publicized due to the politics of the issue)

Here is where the issue is more complex… we are not introducing a species.. Cyanobacteria were chosen since they are already part of the environment. And the GMO aspect is generally safe - it is the interface of these two that this project addresses… si a PET eating Se ecologically disruptive when Se is not - and PET has already been introduced and is already disrupting the environment? Is fixing a disruption, a disruption of its own?
